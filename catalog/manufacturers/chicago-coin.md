Chicago Coin Machine Manufacturing Company was one of the most prolific and enduring pinball manufacturers in American history, producing games from the dawn of the coin-operated amusement industry in the early 1930s through the [[technology-generation:solid-state]] transition of the late 1970s. Founded by Sam Wolberg and [[person:sam-gensburg]] in Chicago, the company operated for more than four decades — a span that encompasses nearly the entire arc of classic pinball.

During the [[technology-generation:electromechanical]] era, Chicago Coin was a reliable second-tier manufacturer, never quite matching the market dominance of [[manufacturer:gottlieb]], [[manufacturer:bally]], or [[manufacturer:williams]], but consistently producing games that operators valued for their solid construction and dependable earning power. The company's catalog ranged across pinball, pitch-and-bat games, gun games, and shuffle alleys, reflecting the diversified approach that characterized many mid-century Chicago amusement firms. Designers including [[person:al-schlappa]], [[person:ed-sermonti]], and [[person:jerry-koci]] contributed to a body of work that favored straightforward, accessible gameplay over mechanical extravagance.

Chicago Coin attempted the leap to [[technology-generation:solid-state]] electronics in the late 1970s but managed only a handful of titles before ceasing pinball production around 1977. The company's long run and large catalog — spanning hundreds of models from [[title:charm-2]] in 1932 to late-era electromechanicals like [[title:stampede]] — make it an essential chapter in the story of Chicago's amusement manufacturing corridor, even if its individual machines rarely achieved the fame of its better-known competitors.
